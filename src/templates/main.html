There’s only so much information available on Australia’s largely opaque lobbying system. It takes time, effort, and the right tools to wade through the federal lobbyist register and join the dots. So, we have extracted the data, broken it down, and reshaped into something digestible.

Here we’ll show you just how Australia's 218 lobbyist firms tend to operate. The firms shown below are sized by the number of clients they have. The bigger the circle, the larger the firm.

Tip: mouseover or tap circles to see names

There's quite a lot the lobbyist register doesn’t tell us. It doesn’t tell us precisely which lobbyist is lobbying who. We don’t know what clients are lobbying about, or when they may have be actively meeting with ministers. We are also blind to the actions of in-house lobbyists or peak interest groups.

The lobbyist register also hides the links many individuals have to government. That’s because lobbyists are only required to declare as former government representatives if they were a federal minister, ministerial staffer, parliamentary secretary, public servant or defence force member.

You can see below how many firms employ lobbyists that meet that narrow definition.

This hides people with significant histories in government, including state premiers or ministers, state political staffers, federal opposition or backbench staffers. Guardian Australia went through the backgrounds of all 483 lobbyists and found an extra 75 individuals who had government links, but were not declared as such on the register.

You can see below how many more firms are revealed to have links to government.

Two of the largest lobbying firms in Australia are Barton Deakin and Hawker Britton. You can see below there’s a significant overlap between the two. Roughly 15 companies engage both Barton Deakin and Hawker Britton, including tech giant Amazon, fast food chain McDonalds, and engineering and development multinational LendLease.

Understanding the firms’ background goes a long way in explaining why big companies would hire lobbyists from both. Hawker Britton is traditionally associated with Labor and its staff are largely ex-Labor advisers, politicians, or party officials.

On the other side, Barton Deakin is more closely aligned with the Liberals, staffed by a former chief of staff to Joe Hockey and adviser to John Howard, among others.

Some lobbying firms tend to specialise in working for a particular industry. There are defence-focused lobbyists, or those who work for energy, mining, and resources companies.

Big pharmaceutical players are one of the big buyers of lobbyist services. We identified almost 70 pharmaceutical or health companies currently engaging lobbyists.

One of the firms specialising in this area is opr Health, a broader health communications consultancy that is registered to lobby, and has a separate public affairs business, Parker and Partners. It boasts a former health minister adviser among its staff.
